This project presents a predictive modeling framework for forecasting the peak bloom dates of cherry blossoms using historical phenological and climate data. The core of the analysis is a custom R function, predict_peak_bloom(), designed to generate year-specific bloom predictions for multiple locations by integrating historical bloom records, geographic information, and climate-derived predictors. The function leverages a pre-trained Random Forest model, which was selected for its ability to handle non-linear relationships, interactions between variables, and mixed-type data, providing a robust approach to modeling the complex phenology of cherry blossoms. Hyperparameter tuning indicated that mtry = 2 was optimal, balancing model bias and variance and improving predictive accuracy.

The predict_peak_bloom() function begins by extracting geographic information—latitude, longitude, and altitude—for each location in the dataset. It then computes climate features known to influence flowering phenology, including average winter maximum temperatures (December–February), winter chill days (defined as days with average temperature below a specified threshold), pre-season growing degree days (GDD) from January 1 to March 15, early-season GDD (March 1–15), and average spring temperatures (March–April). These predictors capture both temporal warming trends and local environmental variability, ensuring the model accounts for climatic drivers that affect bloom timing.

To handle missing values or incomplete records in the target year, the function calculates historical averages for each climate metric and fills gaps with these long-term means. This approach ensures that predictions remain robust even when some temperature records are incomplete, maintaining model consistency across years. All predictors are standardized and aligned with the Random Forest model’s factor levels, particularly for location, to guarantee that categorical encoding matches the training data. This careful preprocessing allows the model to generalize to years not present in the original dataset.

Once the predictor dataset is assembled and cleaned, the function applies the Random Forest model to estimate the day-of-year (DOY) of peak bloom. Predicted DOY values are rounded and converted into calendar dates, producing an interpretable output that directly maps onto the growing season. For example, predictions for 2026 yield bloom dates ranging from April 2 in Washington, D.C., to April 7 in Kyoto and New York City, with intermediate dates for Liestal and Vancouver, demonstrating both spatial variation and the influence of local climate conditions.

The methodology integrates historical and recent climatic variability, geographic factors, and non-linear relationships between predictors to produce accurate, location-specific forecasts. The Random Forest approach, combined with comprehensive feature engineering—including winter temperature averages, GDD accumulation, and chill days—enables the model to capture subtle interactions that drive cherry blossom phenology. By focusing on predictive performance and interpretability, this framework provides actionable insights for ecological forecasting, festival planning, and phenological research, demonstrating a scalable and reproducible approach to predicting peak bloom under variable climatic conditions.
